Australia Aquatic Harvest

Seafood plays an important role in the Australian diet. Every year, we consume approximately 15 kg of fish each person, and our hunger is increasing. Our seafood comes from a variety of sources, with approximately 65% of what we eat imported from other countries (Seafood in Australia, n.d.). Australia's fish are brought to the table by the Aquaculture and wild capture production in Australia. However, by global measures, our waters are not extremely productive. Unlike other countries, the Australian continent lacks major offshore upwellings and sufficient runoff from the landmass to produce nutrients that support greater fish species naturally (Hobday et al. 2006) Hence Australia's seafood is sourced from both wild catch and aquaculture.

Aquaculture Seafood Produced in 2022

The expansion of Australian aquaculture has been mostly driven by an increase in salmonid production and a decrease in wild-caught production. In recent years, the aquaculture business has broadened the composition of species produced, with a greater concentration on prawns, abalone, oysters, and finfish variations such as barramundi and kingfish. (Gross Value Production is measured in tonnes and Apparent Consumption is measured in millions)

Fishery gross value of production (GVP) estimates the monetary amount of activity in commercial fisheries and aquaculture production. GVP is computed by multiplying the total weight of production by the landed unit value. The landed unit value is defined as the beach price for fish species obtained in wild-catch fisheries and the farm gate price for aquaculture goods. Beach price is utilised so that GVP measures what is immediately produced from the resource rather than the value added component as the product goes through the supply chain.
Apparent consumption data are given as total product amount each year, and per capita consumption is calculated by dividing apparent consumption by the mean resident population for that year.
